# Potential Climatic Consequences of an Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation Slowdown in the Carpathian Basin

## Project Proposal

### Introduction

The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is one of the most important components of the global climate system. It transports warm surface waters from lower latitudes toward the North Atlantic and returns colder, denser waters southward at depth. This circulation contributes substantially to the climate of Europe by redistributing heat across the Atlantic Ocean.

Recent studies have suggested that the AMOC may weaken during the twenty-first century as a consequence of anthropogenic climate change. Some researchers have proposed that continued weakening could increase the risk of crossing critical thresholds, potentially resulting in abrupt changes in climate patterns. Public discussion of AMOC weakening often focuses on cooling in Western Europe, particularly regions bordering the North Atlantic. However, considerably less attention has been given to inland regions such as the Carpathian Basin.

### Why the Carpathian Basin Matters

The Carpathian Basin occupies a unique geographical position within Central Europe. Surrounded by the Carpathian Mountains, the Alps, and the Dinaric mountain system, it exhibits climatic characteristics that differ from those of both Atlantic Europe and continental Eastern Europe. The basin contains much of present-day Hungary and extends into neighboring countries.

Because of its enclosed topography and mixed continental, Atlantic, and Mediterranean influences, the climatic response of the Carpathian Basin to AMOC weakening may differ substantially from that of Western Europe. Understanding these differences is important for regional climate adaptation, agriculture, water management, and ecosystem planning.

### Why AMOC Weakening is Relevant

Most projections of future European climate assume continued greenhouse-gas-driven warming. However, AMOC weakening has the potential to alter atmospheric circulation patterns, temperature distributions, precipitation regimes, and extreme weather events across Europe.

While Western Europe may experience reduced warming or regional cooling under some AMOC slowdown scenarios, it remains unclear whether similar responses would occur within the Carpathian Basin. The region may experience a combination of continued warming, altered precipitation patterns, increased drought risk, and modified seasonal temperature variability. Investigating these possibilities is necessary for understanding future climate risks in Central Europe.

### Research Question

How does the projected climatic response of the Carpathian Basin differ from that of Western Europe under scenarios involving substantial AMOC weakening?

### Hypothesis

The Carpathian Basin experiences a weaker cooling response than Western Europe under AMOC weakening scenarios because continental climate influences and regional topography partially offset the reduction in Atlantic heat transport.

### Climate Variables to be Studied

The study will analyze:

1. Mean annual surface air temperature
2. Winter temperature anomalies
3. Summer temperature anomalies
4. Total annual precipitation
5. Seasonal precipitation patterns
6. Drought-related indicators
7. Atmospheric circulation indicators where available

These variables will be compared between the Carpathian Basin and Western Europe using observational datasets and climate model simulations.

### Expected Contribution

This project aims to evaluate whether the Carpathian Basin should be considered a distinct climate-response region under AMOC weakening scenarios. By focusing on an inland Central European basin rather than the Atlantic-facing regions commonly discussed in the literature, the study seeks to provide a more regionally specific assessment of future climate risks.
